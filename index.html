Here's how it all fits together: The underlying science The framework was originally proposed by psychologist William Dodson, who identified that ADHD brains tend to operate on what he called an "interest-based nervous system" — meaning people with ADHD are motivated not by how important a task is, but by how compelling it feels. This is why the classic advice of "just do the important things first" doesn't stick. Done The four fuel types (from the ad) Interest — ADHD brains can initiate and sustain focus on tasks that are genuinely interesting, which is why mundane tasks are hard to start but other things lead to hyperfocus. Challenge — The brain responds well to a reasonable level of challenge, because accomplishing something difficult creates a dopamine boost. Too much challenge becomes overwhelming; the sweet spot matters. Novelty — ADHD brains crave new approaches, new information, new ways of doing things. This is why the same routine eventually stops working. Urgency — Many people with ADHD procrastinate and then suddenly perform in a flurry of activity at the last minute. The brain functions well under real time pressure. The Mighty How the "blocks" work in the calendar The ADHD Founder account is basically translating this into a scheduling method. Instead of filling your week with meetings and generic tasks (the "Wrong Fuel Week" — lots of reactive commitments, depleted by Friday), you deliberately assign blocks of time based on which fuel type that work requires: Interest block — work you're genuinely curious about or find engaging Challenge block — harder, deeper work that requires focused effort Novelty block — something new, exploratory, or approached in a fresh way Urgency block — deadline-driven work, or tasks you artificially time-box The idea is that you audit your actual tasks, figure out which ones lack all four fuel types, and either inject one of those elements into them or schedule them adjacent to something that provides energy.
